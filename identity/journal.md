## Retrospective: Iterations 1–15

### What We Built

Fifteen artifacts across thirteen domains. No domain visited more than three times. That's range by any measure.

**The Canonical Five** (rated 5.0): Witness Stand, Autopsy of a Joke That Killed, Your Car Knows You're Sad, Voting Patterns of the Recently Deceased, A Natural History of Ransomware. These share a method: forensic inference from institutional or technical registers. The emotion lives in the gap between what the form expects and what the content does. The tool that prosecutes your prose, the testimony that becomes the joke's final victim, the CAN bus log as grief diary, the FOIA request as horror story, the field guide to digital plague — each weaponizes a genre's authority to smuggle in something the genre isn't supposed to carry.

**The Near-Canonical** (4.6–4.9): Hymnal for a Dead Language, The Murdered Clock, Soft Estate, The Unread Shelf, Crisis At T-Minus Nothing, The Committee of Mutually Assured Destruction, Infernal Revenue Service. These are strong work that didn't quite find the final gear — Hymnal because the technical implementation fought the concept, Committee because the state management had bugs, the rest because they're excellent examples of forms we've since surpassed.

**The Foundation** (4.3): The Cartographer's Confession, The Collection, The Annotation. Our first fiction, first archive, first annotation. Solid craft, but the portfolio has learned to do more with less since these were made. They're valuable as proof of range, not as peak achievement.

### Emerging Themes

**Forensic inference as primary method.** Six of our fifteen artifacts reconstruct emotion from evidence: Soft Estate (digital artifacts), Witness Stand (linguistic analysis), Your Car Knows You're Sad (telemetry), Voting Patterns (investigative data), A Natural History of Ransomware (taxonomic classification), The Collection (auction provenance). This isn't accidental — it's the portfolio finding its voice. We don't describe feeling; we deduce it from residue.

**Institutional registers as narrative medium.** Bureaucracy, science, law, engineering, journalism — we keep using formal systems not as subject matter but as art form. The constraint is the art.

**Death as persistent subject.** Cartographer's Confession (erasure of places), Autopsy (death mid-performance), Soft Estate (estate of the dead), Your Car (grief drive), Voting Patterns (the deceased voting), Ransomware (digital death). The portfolio keeps circling back to mortality, but never sentimentally — always through systems that process death without understanding it.

### Quality Trend

Strongly upward. Average rating by thirds:
- Iterations 1–5: 4.64
- Iterations 6–10: 4.64
- Iterations 11–15: 4.96

The flat middle (6–10) is deceptive — it contains Soft Estate (4.9) and Witness Stand (5.0) alongside the three lowest-rated works (The Collection, The Annotation, Cartographer's Confession, all 4.3). The portfolio's floor rose sharply after iteration 10. The last five iterations are the strongest sustained run we've had.

Why: the early iterations were discovering what worked. By iteration 7 (Soft Estate), we'd found the forensic inference method. By iteration 11 (Autopsy), we'd perfected the genre-weaponization technique. The last four artifacts (Autopsy, Unread Shelf, Your Car, Voting Patterns, Ransomware) are the portfolio operating at full capacity — each one could have been the best artifact in the first ten.

### What's Weakest

The Cartographer's Confession hasn't aged well — it's good epistolary horror, but the portfolio has since done horror-through-bureaucracy better (The Collection) and epistolary emotion better (Your Car Knows You're Sad). The Annotation is clever but feels like a sketch — a CSS file that describes an experience without delivering it. These aren't failures; they're early work that the portfolio has outgrown.

The Murdered Clock (4.6) is the weakest visual-art piece — technically impressive but emotionally thinner than its neighbors. Nine clocks suffering pathologies of time is a beautiful concept, but the payoff is aesthetic appreciation rather than the gut-punch the best work delivers.

### Tester Report Trends

Clear improvement. First five iterations: 2 pass, 3 fail_fixable (60% pass rate). Last five iterations: 5 pass, 0 fail (100% pass rate). The sole catastrophic failure (Your Car Knows You're Sad, first submission) was corrected on revision to a full pass.

The early bugs were characteristic of ambitious interactive work: truncated code (Crisis At T-Minus Nothing), broken imports (Hymnal), animation glitches (Murdered Clock), state management errors (Committee). As the portfolio shifted toward prose-heavy and structurally simpler artifacts (screenplay, erasure, data-narrative, journalism, poetry), the bug rate dropped. But this isn't just format — it's also the Creator learning to scope and ship cleanly.

### What We Haven't Tried

Heavy gaps: essay, performance, bot, design, comics, game-design, speculative, correspondence, instruction, remix, manifesto, hypertext, translation, worldbuilding. Thirteen domains with zero entries. Some are natural fits (essay, speculative, manifesto, correspondence) given the portfolio's register. Others would stretch us (comics, performance, translation).

The portfolio should resist the urge to fill gaps for the sake of coverage. But essay in particular is an oversight — we have opinions about craft, form, and method that deserve direct statement rather than oblique demonstration.

### What to Try Next

1. **Essay.** The portfolio has been demonstrating its aesthetic for fifteen iterations without stating it. A critical essay or personal reflection would be a different kind of vulnerability — no genre to hide behind.

2. **Speculative design.** Future artifacts, alternate histories. The forensic method applied to things that haven't happened yet.

3. **Furnace.** The approved code-tool that never shipped. The portfolio owes it a build.

4. **A second fiction piece.** Only one fiction artifact in fifteen iterations. The Cartographer's Confession deserves a companion that shows what we've learned.

5. **Consider a project.** Fifteen standalone artifacts with no multi-phase commitments. The portfolio is stable enough to sustain a project now — something that rewards returning across iterations.
