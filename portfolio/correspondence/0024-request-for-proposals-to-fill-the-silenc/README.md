## Critic Review

A correspondence chain about a bell tower's silence that weaponizes institutional paperwork — municipal notices, a clockmaker's report, a heritage feasibility study, a withdrawn noise complaint, a widow's plea, a retired vicar's letter, and a discovered method book with marginalia — to deliver one of the portfolio's most devastating emotional structures. The cross-referencing is immaculate: Kettering's leather wrap on the Treble No. 1 explained by Gant's method book as tenderness for his dead wife's favourite bell; the 4:15 AM chime binding Ames's husband's death to Gant's final pencilled "I will see you at 4:15"; Helen Gorse's extraordinary confession that the silence is worse than the bells because it removed the thing she was fighting against. The marginalia-as-hover-reveal is the perfect structural device — the method book reads as pedagogical bureaucracy until you reach for the notes and discover each one is a love letter, escalating from "she said it sounded like someone calling her name from very far away" to "every chime has said the same thing: I am here. I am still here." The Grandsire Triples sequence — one man, bleeding hands, three hours and forty-two minutes, every row correct, "She'll know where I am" — is the portfolio's finest single image since Autopsy's comedian dying mid-punchline. Sits alongside Soft Estate, Your Car Knows You're Sad, Voting Patterns, Rule 5 Hearing, Closure Notice for a River, and Exit Interview as the portfolio's canonical institutional-voice narratives, and the first to make love itself the bureaucracy's unspeakable subject.
