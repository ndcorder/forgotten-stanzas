VI. PROFESSIONAL ASSESSMENT — K. HOFFMANN

I am required by Language Services Division protocol to provide an
assessment of the incident, including any factors that may have
contributed to the interpreter's failure to render service.

I have reviewed the source audio. I have reviewed the transcript
of the Speaker's complete statement, including the informal remarks
appended to his prepared text.

The Speaker's prepared remarks were within standard difficulty range
for Security Council consultations. Vocabulary was consistent with
MINURSO-related discussions. Sentence structure was moderately complex
but well within the capacity of any Staff-level interpreter.

The Speaker's informal closing remarks contained a figurative
expression drawn from a regional dialect. This expression is not
included in standard Spanish-language reference materials. It does not
appear in the UN terminology database. I was unable to locate it in
the Dictionary of the Royal Spanish Academy or in three regional
dialectal supplements I consulted.

I was, however, able to identify the component words of the
expression. I will not reproduce them here.

The component words, taken together, describe a physical structure
associated with containment. The word for this structure is common
across several Spanish dialects. The modifying word — I believe it to
be a regional or archaic term — appears to describe a quality of
sound. Specifically: the quality of sound when it is

[Entry deleted by preparer.]

I have heard this expression once before. It was not in a
professional context. It was spoken by a woman from the same region
as the Speaker. She was elderly. She was describing a place from her
childhood — a place that, by her account, no longer existed. She was
describing it to her granddaughter, who was asking about a family
photograph. The grandmother became very quiet. She said the phrase.
Then she said: "There are words that are not for translating. There
are words that are only for carrying."

I believe Elena Vargas recognized the Speaker's expression. I believe
she recognized it not as the diplomatic figure of speech the Speaker
intended, but as something else — something the Speaker did not know
he was saying. I believe she understood that the expression, in its
original context, does not mean what the Speaker believed it to mean.
I believe she understood that the Speaker had learned this phrase from
someone who knew its original meaning, and that he had inherited it
without inheriting the knowledge of what it described.

I believe she chose not to translate it.

I do not believe this was a failure of interpretation.

I believe it was a decision about what language is for.

I understand that this assessment may not satisfy the requirements
of this form.
