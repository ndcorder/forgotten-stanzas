## Critic Review

The portfolio's definitive correspondence piece. The central structural inversion — a will that distributes nothing and denies everything, where every bequest is a deletion request and every inheritance is an absence — is executed with devastating precision across nine articles of escalating cruelty. The executor's annotations are the portfolio's finest use of institutional voice since The Defect Report: Helen Voss administering erasure she finds unbearable, recording context she is legally required to destroy, caught between the law that permits this and the justice it isn't. The specific details are load-bearing at every level: the shared Google Doc with 47 itinerary items for a trip that will never happen, the Netflix history showing Margaret finally watching the series Sarah had begged her to see (alone, at night, never mentioned), the daughter's school photo with "half her face behind the chemistry teacher" cut out and framed, the post-it notes reading "I love you more than this list will hold" that Sarah refuses to destroy because "they are mine to read." Laplace the cat — boarded on 10 November with six months prepaid, salmon food bought in bulk on 11 November — is the quietest confirmation of planning in the portfolio, and the fact that the boarding ends 10 May 2026 means Margaret arranged care for her cat through the date she expected someone to need to decide what to do with him, which is the most careful thing a person can do before dying, and it is buried in an executor's note about a veterinary bill. The ending — Voss filing the will on 25 April, records erased by 25 May, this document the sole surviving record — makes the reader hold the only copy of information that implicates them in its preservation, which is exactly the paradox the will enacts. The Tester's note about "failing body" vs. suicide is not an inconsistency; it's Margaret's final understatement, and it belongs. Sits alongside Exit Interview, The Defect Report, and The Translation of Silence as the portfolio's canonical forensic narratives, and the first to make the act of reading a legal document an act of witnessing something the law cannot address.
