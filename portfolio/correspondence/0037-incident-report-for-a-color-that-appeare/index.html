At approximately 0932 hours on 06 November 2024, this officer responded to a call regarding a chemical release at the ████████████ Industrial Park, Building 14. Upon arrival, the facility had been evacuated. Three employees — identified as witnesses A, B, and C in subsequent statements — reported observing an unusual visual phenomenon during a routine transfer of ███████ between storage containers.

All three witnesses independently described a color associated with the vapor cloud produced during the transfer. None of the witnesses were able to identify or name the color. Each witness provided a distinct set of comparisons and analogies. None of these descriptions match any entry in the standard color reference materials consulted by this officer.

Building 14 was secured pending hazmat assessment. No physical samples of the vapor were collected, as the cloud dissipated within approximately four minutes of the initial release. Security camera footage from the facility's four interior cameras has been reviewed; the relevant footage (Timestamps 0930:12–0934:47) shows the vapor cloud as a grey-white haze of unremarkable appearance. The color described by witnesses is not visible in the footage.

All three witnesses appeared calm, coherent, and cooperative during initial interviews. None exhibited signs of chemical exposure, distress, or disorientation. Toxicology screens administered on-site returned negative for all three individuals.

Witness statements were taken separately and are attached as Documents 02, 03, and 04.

Witness Statement — A

Taken 06 November 2024, 1047 hours

Witness
Dorothy Chen, ██████████
Duration

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Please describe what you saw during the transfer, in your own words.

WITNESS CHEN:

I was at my station, running the panel. The transfer started normal. Pressure was fine, temperature was fine. Then the relief valve on Tank 4 popped, which happens sometimes — it's not a big deal, you just wait for it to reseal. But when the vapor came out, I noticed the color of it. The cloud. It wasn't like anything I've seen before.

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Can you describe the color?

WITNESS CHEN:

That's where it gets difficult. It was warm. Not warm like red or orange is warm — those are bright and aggressive. This was a warm that felt like it came from inside. Like the feeling you get when you hold a mug of something hot and you can feel the heat through the ceramic but you can't see it, you just know it's there. It was the visual equivalent of that feeling.

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

I understand, but I need you to try to be more specific about the color itself. Was it light or dark?

WITNESS CHEN:

Both. Neither. It was light the way an empty room is light — not because of its own brightness, but because it was holding light that came from somewhere else. But it was also dark the way a shadow is dark. I know that doesn't make sense.

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Would you say it was a shade of a known color? A green, a blue, something like that?

WITNESS CHEN:

No. It wasn't any color I have a name for. I've been trying to think of one since it happened. I keep looking at things — at the walls, at the sky, at my clothes — looking for something that matches, and nothing does. It's like a word I almost remember. It's on the tip of my eye.tip of my eye

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

On the tip of your eye?

WITNESS CHEN:

Yes. That's the only way I can describe it.

Witness Statement — B

Taken 06 November 2024, 1215 hours

Witness
Ronald Figueroa, ████████████
Duration
31 minutes
DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Please describe the color of the vapor cloud you witnessed on the morning of November 6th.

WITNESS FIGUEROA:

Are you asking what color it was, or what color it felt like?

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Is there a difference?

WITNESS FIGUEROA:

With this, yeah. With a normal color, no. Red is red. You see it, you name it. But this — it was more like hearing a sound with your eyes. Like a soundsynesthetic? that's close enough to words that you know it's trying to tell you something, but it's not a word you know.

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Was the cloud colored, or did it just create a colored impression?

WITNESS FIGUEROA:

The cloud was colored. Definitely. I could see it right in front of me. But the color wasn't like paint on a wall. It was like — do you know how sometimes, in very early morning, right before the sun comes up, the sky does a color that isn't any of the colors the sky is supposed to be? And for about thirty seconds you see it and you think, "That's a color that doesn't have a name." But then the sun comes up and you forget about it, because it was just the light playing tricks. This was like that, except it wasn't early morning, and it wasn't the light playing tricks. It was a real color and it was right there.

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Can you compare it to any color you know?

WITNESS FIGUEROA:

I've been thinking about that all morning. I work with metal. I know what heat looks like — I know the whole spectrum, from barely-there blue to white-hot to that cherry red you get before the steel becomes unmanageable. This wasn't any of those. But it had the same conviction those colors have. You know how heat colors are the most honest colors there are? They don't pretend to be anything. They just are. This color was like that. Completely honest. I just don't know what it was saying.

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Was it warm-toned or cool-toned?

WITNESS FIGUEROA:

Yes. Both. At the same time. I know that's not possible, but that's what I saw.

Witness Statement — C

Taken 06 November 2024, 1443 hours

Witness
Gerald Petrovak, Petrovak Professional Painting
Duration
38 minutes
Occupation
House painter, 27 years professional experience
Presence at site
Contracted work, adjacent unit (Bldg 15)
DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Mr. Petrovak, thank you for coming in. I understand you were working in the building next to the incident site. Can you tell me what you saw?

WITNESS PETROVAK:

I was prepping a ceiling — scraping, you know, the usual. The bay door was open because it gets hot in there with the pressure washer running. I saw the vapor come out through the gap between the buildings. Just a wisp of it, the edge of the cloud. But the color stopped me.

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Stopped you how?

WITNESS PETROVAK:

I got down off my ladder. I walked to the bay door. I stood there and looked at it. I must have been standing there for two, three minutes. I couldn't stop looking. I've been mixing and matching colors professionally for twenty-seven years. I can look at a wall and tell you the Benjamin Moore number. I can tell you the difference between White Dove OC-17 and Simply White OC-117 in my sleep. I know ochre. I know sienna. I know burnt and raw and every earth tone from here to Sunday. This was not any of those.

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Can you place it in a color family?

WITNESS PETROVAK:

No. And I've tried.27 years No. That's what's been eating at me. I've sat with my swatch book for hours. I've been through every chip, every fan deck, every sample card I own. I do color consultations, Detective. People pay me to tell them what color something is. I have never once in my career been unable to match a color. I have matched colors from photographs, from memory, from a customer's vague description of their grandmother's kitchen curtains. And I am telling you: I cannot place this color. It is not in any family I know. It's not in the spectrum. It's not between any two colors. It is not a mixture, a shade, a tint, or a tone of anything I have ever seen.

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Was it similar to any color you've mixed?

WITNESS PETROVAK:

It was similar to the moment before a color becomes a color. You know what I mean? When you're mixing paint and you're turning the stir stick and for a split second the blend is something that doesn't exist yet — it's not the two colors you started with, and it's not the color you're going to end up with. It's a color that only exists in the transition. It's there for a heartbeat and then it's gone. This was that color, but it held still. It stayed. It was right in front of me, permanent and complete, and it was still a color that doesn't exist.

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Is there anything else you can tell us about it?

WITNESS PETROVAK:

[Long pause.] It was the most beautiful color I've ever seen. And I'm not a sentimental man. I don't say things like that. But it was. It was so beautiful it made my work feel like lying. Every color I have ever put on a wall — and I have put color on thousands of walls — felt like a dishonest version of that one. [Pause.] I'd like to stop talking about it now.

DETECTIVE CALLOWAY:

Of course. Thank you, Mr. Petrovak.

Dear Detective Calloway,

Thank you for forwarding the witness statements from Case 2024-HA-0771. I have read each of them — including the follow-up interviews — with considerable interest, and I will be honest with you: I am not certain what I can offer. But I will try.

You asked me to identify the color described by the three witnesses. I must begin by noting that I cannot identify a color from descriptions alone. I can, however, assess the consistency and plausibility of the descriptions.

What strikes me is the convergence. Three unrelated individuals, with no apparent opportunity to coordinate their accounts, each describe a visual experience that resists color vocabulary in the same fundamental way. Ms. Chen describes it in terms of feeling (warmth through ceramic, a word on the tip of her eye). Mr. Figueroa describes it in terms of sound (a voice speaking a language he doesn't know, but communicating clearly). Mr. Petrovak describes it in terms of process (the transitional moment between two colors during mixing). These are wildly different analogies, but they converge on a single structural observation: the color is legible but unreadable.

In color science, we distinguish between perception (what the visual system registers) and categorization (what the cognitive system does with that registration). What your witnesses describe sounds like a perception without a category — not a failure to see, but a failure to file. The visual system received data. The cognitive system could not process it.

This should not be possible. The human visual system is constrained to the visible spectrum (approximately 380–700nm). We perceive combinations of wavelengths within this range as colors. Every combination produces a color that can be named or, at minimum, described by its relation to other colors. There is no combination of visible wavelengths that should produce an "unnameable" color.

There are exceptions.?? I say "should" because the science of color perception is not complete. There are theoretical colors — combinations of cone-cell responses that cannot be produced by any single physical stimulus — that exist in our mathematical models but have never been observed in practice. They are sometimes called "impossible colors." Your witnesses' descriptions are consistent with an impossible-color experience, but I want to be very clear: this is speculative. I have no evidence for this claim. I am telling you what the descriptions resemble, not what caused them.

I recommend spectral analysis of the site and any residue from the vapor cloud. If the color has a physical basis, spectroscopy will reveal it. If it does not —

I don't know how to finish that sentence, Detective. I have never had to finish that sentence before.

I am available for further consultation as needed.

This memorandum constitutes the closing statement for Case 2024-HA-0771, concerning the industrial vapor release at Building 14, ████████████ Industrial Park, on 06 November 2024.

The incident has been documented in full. No injuries were sustained. No hazardous materials were released beyond acceptable thresholds. The facility has been cleared for normal operations. By every metric available to this department, the case is closed.

I am not satisfied.

I have spent five weeks trying to write a report about a color. I have taken six statements, consulted a specialist, and commissioned a laboratory analysis. I have filled out every form correctly. I have followed every procedure. The file is complete.

The file does not contain what happened.

Three people saw something. They saw it clearly and independently. They described it with the specific frustration of people who are not confused but who are failing at a task that should not be difficult. They were not hallucinating. They were not under chemical influence. They were not coached. I have been a detective for fourteen years. I know when someone is telling me what they saw.

They saw a color that is not in this file.

I have no conclusion.—MC Not because the photographs show nothing unusual — though they don't. Not because the spectrophotometer failed — it worked perfectly. Not because the witnesses are unreliable — they are the most reliable part of this investigation. The color is not in this file because this file is made of language, and the color is not in language.

Dr. Voss suggested that the witnesses may have experienced an "impossible color" — a combination of cone-cell responses that the visual system can generate mathematically but that no physical stimulus should produce. This is a theory. It cannot be confirmed. You cannot confirm a theory about a color you cannot show to anyone.

I will note, for the record, that I have read these witness statements many times. I have read them in the evidence room, under fluorescent light, with the door closed. I have tried to see the color the way they describe it — the warmth Chen described, the conviction Figueroa described, the transitional qualityPetrovak Petrovak described. I have looked at the space on the page where the color would be, if a page could hold a color.

I understand that this memorandum will be filed and that the case will be marked resolved. I understand that "no evidence of anomaly" is the official and correct finding. I am not disputing the finding. I am noting, for the record, that "no evidence of anomaly" and "nothing happened" are not the same sentence.

Three people saw a color that should not exist. They saw it clearly, completely, and with total confidence. The only evidence that remains is the absence of evidence — seven documents that collectively describe the shape of something they cannot contain.

The case is closed.

The color is not.
