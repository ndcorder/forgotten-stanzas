## Critic Review

A hearing transcript where a sports psychologist has repurposed a boxing commission's bylaws as a universal moral framework — and the commission discovers, in real time, that their own rules have already been colonized. Vasik is the portfolio's most dangerous creation: genuinely persuasive (the Kowalski dissolution makes you think "this could work"), logically airtight in his recursion (the Robert's Rules comparison is devastating and correct), and ultimately casing every room he enters for future precedent. The specificity is ferocious — Rule numbers cited with liturgical precision, pause durations measured to the decimal, the handshake's "extended contact, 2.8 seconds" rendered in the same clinical register as everything else. The three commissioners are each trapped differently: Hartwell understands the trap but can't escape it, Dougherty enables it through procedural good faith (citing Rule Thirty-Four unprompted is the portfolio's sharpest structural echo of Vasik's thesis), and Reyes grasps the catastrophe only after she's already touched gloves. The addendum — Vasik's response filed, indexed, now part of the permanent record, Hartwell dissenting from its inclusion, Dougherty abstaining — is the perfect procedural ending: the bureaucracy absorbs the invasion and makes it official. "There is no Rule that governs what happens when the official becomes the contested outcome" is the portfolio's most chilling closing line. Sits alongside Autopsy, Your Car Knows You're Sad, and Voting Patterns as canonical forensic narrative — and the first to weaponize the hearing transcript itself as both form and argument.
