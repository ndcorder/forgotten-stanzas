**REYES:** Good morning. This hearing is called to order at nine oh four AM, November fourteenth, 2024. Dr. Vasik, thank you for appearing. Before we begin, will you state your full name and affiliation for the record?

**VASIK:** Emil Andreas Vasik. I retired from the University of New Mexico's Department of Sports Psychology in 2019. I maintain a private consulting practice.

**REYES:** Dr. Vasik, I'm going to be direct. This commission received a complaint three weeks ago from Judge Angela Molina of the Second Judicial District. She presided over a custody modification hearing in which opposing counsel submitted your Protocol as a framework for determining quote "fair play" in the dissolution of a marriage. **VASIK:** I'm aware of the filing.

**REYES:** You're aware of it because you wrote the section that cited our bylaws.

**VASIK:** That's correct.

**HARTWELL:** Dr. Vasik. I've read your Protocol cover to cover twice now. I want to make sure I understand what it is before we get to what it's been used for. In your words — what is the Cognitive Refereeing Protocol?

**VASIK:** It's a decision-making framework. It applies the judgment principles of officiated athletic competition to non-athletic contexts. The core insight is this: referees in combat sports make consequential moral decisions in real time under conditions of incomplete information. That's not unlike what judges, mediators, therapists, and clergy do every day. The Protocol systematizes what officials do intuitively.

**HARTWELL:** Systematizes.

**VASIK:** Yes.

**HARTWELL:** So when a boxing referee determines whether a clinch is excessive, or whether a low blow was intentional — you believe that judgment can be abstracted into a general-purpose decision procedure.

**VASIK:** I don't believe it. I've demonstrated it. Over six hundred applications in the past three years.

**REYES:** Commissioner Dougherty.

**DOUGHERTY:** Dr. Vasik, I want to understand the scope before I form an opinion. You say six hundred applications. In what contexts?

**VASIK:** Initially in sports-adjacent settings — contract negotiations, NIL disputes, locker room mediation. But the principles generalize. After the first year, I began accepting referrals from family court, pastoral counseling, HR departments, and — yes — one funeral home.

**DOUGHERTY:** A funeral home.

**VASIK:** A dispute between siblings about whether their father's service should include an open casket. The younger sister wanted it. The older brother did not. They'd been arguing for three days. The funeral director called me.

**DOUGHERTY:** And you applied boxing regulations to a father's funeral.

**VASIK:** I applied a decision framework derived from the principles that govern fair contest to a situation where two parties were in conflict and both believed they were right. Rule Five — fair play — asks whether both participants have equal opportunity to participate in the contest. I asked each sibling what their father would have wanted. The sister had no answer. The brother produced a handwritten note from 2016. I ruled for the brother. The service was closed casket. Both siblings shook hands afterward.

**REYES:** Commissioner Hartwell.

**HARTWELL:** You ruled.

**VASIK:** I made a recommendation that both parties accepted.

**HARTWELL:** You "ruled" for the brother. Because he produced evidence. In a funeral home. Using a framework derived from the Marquess of Queensberry Rules.

**VASIK:** Derived from principles. The Marquess of Queensberry Rules are one expression of those principles. Your commission's bylaws are another. The Protocol draws from the bylaws directly. Rule Five specifically.

**HARTWELL:** I want to come back to Rule Five. But first — the funeral. When you told the sister she couldn't see her father one last time, what specific rule governed that?

**VASIK:** Rule Five doesn't stand alone. It's interpreted in conjunction with Rule Twelve. Governing evidence and the burden of proof. The sister's claim was unsupported. The brother's was documented.

**HARTWELL:** Rule Twelve of our bylaws. The rule that governs whether a knockout was the result of a legal blow.

**VASIK:** The rule that governs how officials evaluate the validity of contested outcomes. Yes.

**HARTWELL:** Commissioner Reyes, for the record — Rule Twelve pertains specifically to the designation of a technical knockout versus a disqualification result. It has never been applied outside a ring.

**REYES:** Dr. Vasik, I think you can see our concern. This commission's bylaws govern athletic competition within this state. Specifically boxing and martial arts. They were drafted by athletic commissioners, approved by the state legislature, and they are designed to protect the safety of combat sport participants. They are not a general-purpose moral framework. You seem to have repurposed them as one.

**VASIK:** With respect, Commissioner — I've read the bylaws more carefully than most of the people who drafted them. Rule One states that the commission exists to "ensure the integrity and safety of all sanctioned contests within its jurisdiction." The word "contest" is not defined in your bylaws as exclusively athletic.

**REYES:** It's defined by common usage and by the enabling statute, which limits this commission's authority to combat sports.

**VASIK:** The enabling statute says "sanctioned contests of athletic competition." But Rule One's language is broader. It says "all sanctioned contests." And Rule Five — fair play and manly behaviour — doesn't mention athletics at all. It's a moral standard. You wrote a moral standard into a regulatory document and you're surprised someone applied it morally.

[PAUSE — 6.1 SECONDS]

**REYES:** Commissioner Dougherty.

**DOUGHERTY:** Dr. Vasik, I want to step back. You've used this Protocol in divorce proceedings.

**VASIK:** Several times.

**DOUGHERTY:** Walk me through a specific example.

**VASIK:** The Kowalski dissolution, 2022. Third judicial district. Husband filed on grounds of irreconcilable differences. Wife contested. The mediator brought me in as a consultant. Both parties had accused the other of — among other things — emotional cruelty. The question before the mediator was whether either party had conducted themselves fairly during the marriage.

**DOUGHERTY:** Fairly.

**VASIK:** Fairly. I applied Rule Five. Fair play requires that no participant gain advantage through conduct that would be considered unsportsmanlike in a contest. In boxing, that's rabbit punches, headbutting, hitting below the belt. I asked both parties to enumerate the behaviors they considered unfair. The husband listed — among other things — his wife's refusal to attend his mother's funeral. The wife listed his decision to sell a jointly-owned vehicle without her knowledge.

**DOUGHERTY:** And how did you — how did the Protocol handle that?

**VASIK:** I mapped each behavior to the closest analog in combat sport. Refusal to attend a family funeral maps to Rule Fourteen — refusing to engage. Which is a warning offense. Selling jointly-owned property without consent maps to Rule Eight — fouls that result in immediate point deduction. I scored both parties. The wife had accumulated more warnings than point deductions. The husband had the reverse. I recommended the mediator find in the wife's favor on the grounds of fair play.

**DOUGHERTY:** You gave a man a point deduction for selling a car.

**VASIK:** I found that selling jointly-owned property without notice constitutes a foul under Rule Eight. Yes.

**DOUGHERTY:** Dr. Vasik, these are human beings. A marriage is not a boxing match.

**VASIK:** A boxing match is two people in a prescribed space, following rules they didn't write, judged by a third party who signals when they've been unfair. Tell me how that differs from what was happening in that courtroom.

**HARTWELL:** Because the courtroom has its own rules, Dr. Vasik. Centuries of them. Developed by jurists, not referees.

**VASIK:** And how's that working? The Kowalski dissolution had been stalled for eleven months. My recommendation settled it in an afternoon.

**ADDENDUM — FILED DECEMBER 3, 2024:**

*Dr. Vasik submitted a formal response to the commission's advisory opinion on December 1, 2024. The response was twelve pages long and cited Rules One, Five, Eight, Twelve, Fifteen, Twenty-Two, Twenty-Three, Thirty-Four, and the commission's own hearing transcript. He argued that the advisory opinion, by acknowledging that the commission's rules structure all interactions within the hearing room, constitutes a formal recognition that those rules apply beyond athletic competition. He further argued that Commissioner Reyes's handshake, recorded in the transcript at 10:23 AM, constitutes participation in a sanctioned contest under Rule Fifteen, and that the commission is now a party to the Protocol's application.*

*The response is reproduced in full in Appendix B.*

*Commissioner Hartwell dissented from the decision to include Appendix B in the record. His dissent is noted.*

*Commissioner Dougherty abstained.*

*The response was filed, indexed, and is now part of this commission's permanent record.*
