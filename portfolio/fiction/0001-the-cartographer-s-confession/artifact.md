**FIELD REPORT 47-B**
**Dated:** 14 March 1923
**Submitted by:** Emilia Voss, Junior Cartographer, Third Class
**Subject:** Preliminary survey, Lake Veld region

Per Directive 22, I enclose my initial survey of the Lake Veld watershed, twelve miles northeast of the Pasha River basin. I was accompanied by Corporal Dain and two Meridian guides, who declined to enter the valley proper, citing local custom. I proceeded alone.

I counted eleven settlements of note in the upper valley, with populations ranging from perhaps one hundred to three hundred persons. These communities appear to be agrarian-pastoral, with complex irrigation channels feeding from the lake. They have established clear territorial boundaries between them, marked by stone cairns at regular intervals.

I have assigned provisional names to each settlement (V-1 through V-11) and noted their positions on the enclosed draft map. The lake itself is roughly five miles in circumference, brackish, and unusually still. No visible inlet or outlet, though the irrigation channels suggest underground sources.

Of note: the valley residents were courteous but distant. They seemed unsurprised by my presence. One elderly woman at V-4 offered water and bread. She pressed my hand and said something I did not understand. My guides later told me she said, "May your ink run clear."

I have marked V-4 for a return visit.

**ENCLOSURE:** Draft Map 47-B (pencil on vellum, 24x36 inches)

**INTERNAL MEMORANDUM**
**To:** All Junior Cartographers
**From:** Director Aldous Crane, Meridian Colonial Survey Office
**Dated:** 2 April 1923
**Re:** Cartographic Standards, Lake Veld Surveys

Colleagues,

A reminder of the principles governing our representation of the Eastern Province:

*   We map for Meridian understanding, not for native self-conception.
*   Where settlements are temporary, seasonal, or below the threshold of administrative relevance (fewer than 50 permanent structures), they may be consolidated or omitted.
*   Where place names are unpronounceable or duplicated across regions, assign designations from the Approved Registry.
*   Roads and paths of negligible strategic or commercial value should be de-emphasized or left unmarked.
*   Natural features of unusual character (springs, groves, formations) should be noted but need not be elaborated unless they bear on resource extraction.

The goal of the Survey is not to replicate what *is* but to create a useful representation of what Meridian governance *requires*. Remember that your work is the foundation upon which the Colonial Office will base its development decisions. Draw accordingly.

A. Crane

*Marginalia in pencil (Voss's hand), written on the reverse side of the memorandum:*

*V-4 is too small, then. Eighteen structures. They won't want it. But the irrigation channels are remarkable. I'll mark it small but I'll keep it.*

**FIELD REPORT 47-D**
**Dated:** 23 May 1923
**Submitted by:** Emilia Voss, Junior Cartographer, Third Class
**Subject:** Return to Lake Veld, detailed survey

I returned to the Lake Veld valley to verify positions and sketch terrain features. I enclose the updated draft.

The valley is much as I left it. The spring planting is well advanced. I spent two nights at V-4, as I hoped. The woman's name is Haret. She has been teaching me words in the valley tongue. They have no written language, she says. "We do not need it. We have the land and the land remembers."

I attempted to explain what a map is. She found the concept deeply amusing. "You make a picture and then the picture tells you where to go?" She laughed until she cried. "The land is already there. You could just walk on it."

I told her that we map so that people who have never walked on the land can know it.

She stopped laughing.

"Why would someone who has never walked here need to know?"

I did not have a satisfactory answer.

I have reverified the positions of all eleven settlements and marked the irrigation channels in detail. The channels are extensive. I have also noted several shrines at the territorial boundaries between communities. They are well maintained, obviously important.

I note for the record that I was forced to omit several features due to space constraints. The map cannot hold everything. I have prioritized settlements, water features, and terrain.

**ENCLOSURE:** Draft Map 47-D (ink on vellum, 24x36 inches)

*Marginalia in pencil (Voss's hand), written in the top margin:*

*The map cannot hold everything.*

*I left off the shrine between V-3 and V-7. I don't know why. It was small. Just a niche in a boulder, with dried flowers and a carved stone. It didn't seem important. But Haret told me later that the boundary shrine keeps the peace. Without it, the two villages forget where one ends and the other begins.*

*I'll put it on the final draft. If there's room.*

**INTERNAL MEMORANDUM**
**To:** Emilia Voss, J.C. III
**From:** Supervisor Ren Hallow, Eastern Province Division
**Dated:** 8 June 1923
**Re:** Draft Map 47-D, Lake Veld

Voss,

Director Crane has reviewed your draft. A few notes:

*   Too many settlements. Consolidate where possible. V-3 and V-7 are too close to distinguish at scale; merge them into a single node.
*   The irrigation channels are unnecessary detail. Show the lake and the river, not the ditches.
*   What is this V-4? Eighteen structures? Below threshold. Remove.
*   V-8, V-9, V-10, V-11 can be generalized as "outlying agricultural activity." We don't need individual nodes.
*   The boundary shrines are picturesque but not cartographically relevant. Omit.
*   Rename the lake. "Veld" is already used for a geographic region in the southern territory. Call it Lake Craven, after the provincial governor.

Revise and resubmit by end of month.

Hallow

*Marginalia in pencil (Voss's hand), written sideways along the left margin:*

*Remove V-4. Remove the shrines. Merge V-3 and V-7. They are not the same village. They speak different dialects. They have been separate for—*

*But he's right. At scale. On the map. They are very close together.*

*I will make the revisions.*

*I will make the revisions.*

**PERSONAL NOTE**
**(Undated, written on the back of a surveyor's supply requisition form)**
**(Estimated date: late June 1923, based on supply records)**

*Before I made the revisions, I wanted to check something. I was bothered by how close V-3 and V-7 were on my draft. I knew they were close in the field, but on the map, they looked like they were overlapping. So I went back to my original field notes.*

*My field notes say V-3 and V-7 are 1.2 miles apart.*

*My draft map says they are 0.4 miles apart.*

*I drew them too close together. I know I did. I remember pacing the distance. I remember writing down 1.2 miles. But when I sat down to draw the map, I drew them closer.*

*I thought about redrawing it correctly. But Hallow's note was on my desk, and it said to merge them. And they were already so close on the draft. And it would be so much work to redo it.*

*So I merged them. I labeled the new node V-3/7. I drew a single set of irrigation channels where there had been two.*

*I felt sick doing it. I don't know why.*

**FIELD REPORT 47-E**
**Dated:** 18 July 1923
**Submitted by:** Emilia Voss, Junior Cartographer, Third Class
**Subject:** Verification of revised Lake Veld map

Per Supervisor Hallow's directive, I returned to the Lake Veld valley to verify my revised draft against the actual terrain.

I am filing this report from Alemere. I returned to the city yesterday evening.

Where V-3 and V-7 had been, there is now a single village. The village is called Vess. It sits between the two old positions, in the no-man's-land where the boundary shrine used to stand. The people there speak a language that is not quite the language of V-3 and not quite the language of V-7, but something in between. They say the village has always been called Vess. They say their grandfathers are buried in the cemetery on the hill.

There is no boundary shrine. There is no boulder with a niche. There are no dried flowers. I asked about it. They didn't know what I was talking about.

Haret was waiting for me at V-4. She asked if I had finished my picture. I told her I had revised it. She looked at me for a long time and said, "You did something to V-3 and V-7."

I told her I had drawn them as one village on my map, because my superiors required it. She went very still.

"But you came back," she said. "You came back to check."

I said yes.

"Then maybe it is not finished," she said. "Maybe the picture is still settling."

I asked her what she meant. She shook her head. "I don't know how your pictures work. We never had them. We never needed them." She gripped my arm. "Please be careful with your ink."

I am requesting a meeting with Supervisor Hallow.

*Marginalia in ink (Voss's hand), written in the bottom margin in cramped handwriting:*

*I went to the archives. I found the original field report from the first Meridian expedition to Lake Veld. Dated 1889. The explorer listed the settlements he encountered.*

*He listed V-3 and V-7 as separate villages, 1.2 miles apart, with a boundary shrine between them.*

*He listed twelve settlements. Not eleven. Twelve.*

*I counted eleven in my first survey. I have reviewed my notes obsessively. I listed eleven. I have no memory of a twelfth settlement.*

*But there is a name on his list that isn't on mine.*

*Tessam. He placed it on the western shore of the lake. I have been to the western shore. It is empty. There is nothing there but tall grass and a pebbled beach. I asked Haret if she knew the name Tessam. She looked away and said, "There are names we do not use anymore."*

*I went back further. I found an earlier survey—a Meridian naval chart from 1847. It lists fourteen settlements around the lake.*

**INTERNAL MEMORANDUM**
**To:** Ren Hallow, Supervisor, Eastern Province Division
**From:** Emilia Voss, J.C. III
**Dated:** 20 July 1923
**Subject:** Lake Veld Map Revisions — Request for Clarification

Supervisor Hallow,

I am writing to request clarification regarding the revision process for the Lake Veld map.

During my verification visit, I noticed discrepancies between my field notes and the terrain as currently constituted. Specifically, features I had consolidated on the revised draft appear to have consolidated in the field as well. I would like to understand whether the Survey Office has received reports of similar discrepancies from other cartographers working in the Eastern Province.

Additionally, I am concerned about the removal of V-4 from the draft. During my visits to the valley, I established a relationship with residents of this community. I would like to request that it be reinstated.

I am aware that this request is unusual.

Respectfully,
Emilia Voss

**INTERNAL MEMORANDUM**
**To:** Emilia Voss, J.C. III
**From:** Ren Hallow, Supervisor, Eastern Province Division
**Dated:** 22 July 1923
**Re:** RE: Lake Veld Map Revisions — Request for Clarification

Voss,

Are you asking me whether the map is changing the territory?

Maps represent. They do not create. If you're confused about what you saw in the field, I suggest you need more sleep.

The Lake Veld map is due at the printer by September. Submit the final draft with the revisions I requested. If V-4 is not on it, I will add it myself.

Hallow

**PERSONAL NOTE**
**(Undated, written in a surveyor's field notebook, pages 14–31, dense handwriting covering every available space)**
**(Estimated date: early August 1923)**

*I tested it. God forgive me, I tested it.*

*I made a small map. Just a test. I drew the valley from memory on a sheet of ordinary paper. I included all eleven settlements—the current eleven. I drew it as carefully as I could.*

*Then I took an eraser and I removed V-11. A small village on the southeastern slope. Maybe forty people. I had not visited it. I did not know anyone there.*

*I waited.*

*The next morning, I checked the survey reports. Corporal Dain's patrol passed through the southeastern slope on the 4th. I asked him about the villages he'd seen.*

*He listed ten.*

*I said, "What about the one on the slope? The small one near the tree line?"*

*He looked confused. "There's nothing on the slope, ma'am. Just pasture."*

*I went to the valley. I drove the motor car as fast as it would go. I went to the southeastern slope.*

*There is nothing there. Just grass.*

*I found Haret at V-4. I was shaking. I think she could see it.*

*I said, "Did you know there was a village on the southeastern slope?"*

*She said, "There was. Once. A long time ago. Before the first surveyors came from the coast."*

*I said, "Before?"*

*She said, "There were more of us once. Before your people started making pictures of us."*

*I told her about the eraser. About V-11. I was crying. I am not proud of this.*

*She put her hand on my arm and said, "You are the first one who came back to ask. You are the first one who tried to put it back."*

*I asked if I could put it back. If I drew it again, would it return?*

*She didn't answer for a long time. Then she said, "The people were there. And then they were not. And I do not know where they went."*

**Dated:** 19 August 1923
**Submitted by:** Emilia Voss, Junior Cartographer, Third Class
**Subject:** Final survey, Lake Veld

I am submitting my final draft of the Lake Veld map with this report.

I have included all settlements currently extant in the valley. I have drawn them as accurately as I can. I have not omitted any settlement that I know to exist.

I want to note, for the record, that I have serious concerns about the revision process. I believe that omissions on Meridian survey maps may have consequences that are not currently understood by this office. I am requesting a formal inquiry.

I have also included, as an enclosure, a separate sheet listing all settlements that have appeared on previous surveys but which I was unable to locate during my visits. I believe this list is incomplete.

**PERSONAL NOTE**
**(Dated 26 August 1923, written in surveyor's field notebook, page 45, handwriting increasingly unsteady)**

*I went to Haret. I ran. I took the motor car as far as the valley road and then I ran.*

*She was in her garden. She saw my face and she put down her tools.*

*I said, "They're removing V-4. They're erasing it from the map. The final map. It goes to the printer tomorrow."*

*She was quiet for a long time. Then she said, "Then we are already gone."*

*I said, "No. I won't let them. I'll steal the map. I'll destroy it."*

*She shook her head. "It isn't the paper. Someone has decided we are not there. The paper is just how they tell the world."*

*She pressed something into my hand. A small carved stone from the shrine between V-3 and V-7. The shrine that doesn't exist anymore.*

*She said, "When you can't see us anymore, hold this. The land remembers even when the picture forgets."*

*I put it in my pocket. I could feel it there, warm from her hand.*

*I am writing this in the motor car. I am going to break into the Survey Office tonight and steal the map. I don't know if it will help. But I cannot sit here and wait for tomorrow.*

**INCIDENT REPORT**
**Filed by:** Night Watchman Paul Drey, Meridian Colonial Survey Office
**Dated:** 27 August 1923
**Subject:** Unauthorized entry, third floor survey office

At approximately 2:00 AM on the morning of 27 August, I discovered an unauthorized person on the third floor of the Survey Office. The individual was identified as Miss Emilia Voss, a junior cartographer employed by this office.

Miss Voss had entered the building using her key. She was found in the drafting room standing over Map 47-F (Lake Veld region). She had torn the map into several pieces and was attempting to burn them in a wastebasket.

I detained her and called the duty officer. Miss Voss was in a state of considerable distress. She stated that she was "trying to save the village" and that "the map will erase them." She was unable to provide a coherent explanation for her actions.

The damaged map has been collected and filed as evidence. Miss Voss was escorted from the building and placed in the custody of the Alemere municipal constabulary.

I recommend that Miss Voss's building access be revoked pending review.

---

**INTERNAL MEMORANDUM**
**To:** Director Aldous Crane
**From:** Ren Hallow, Supervisor, Eastern Province Division
**Dated:** 28 August 1923
**Re:** Emilia Voss — Lake Veld Map

Director Crane,

Regarding the incident of 27 August:

Voss has been suspended from duty. I have retrieved the damaged map and am having it reconstructed by the drafting department. We should be able to send it to the printer by the end of the week with minimal delay.

I believe Voss has been under considerable strain. Her recent reports have included references to "disappearing villages" and "consequences of representation" that suggest a break with reality. Her fieldwork was otherwise competent, and I understand she performed adequately prior to the Lake Veld assignment.

I recommend she be offered medical leave.

Regarding the map: I have reviewed her accusations and find them without merit. The Lake Veld map is a standard representation of the region and conforms to all cartographic guidelines. There is nothing unusual about it.

Hallow

*Postscript in Crane's hand: "Approved. Send her to the sanatorium if she'll go. Print the map."*

**PERSONAL NOTE**
**(Dated 30 August 1923, written in pencil on the back of a medical intake form, Alemere Sanatorium)**

*They took the map. They are printing it. V-4 is not on it. The irrigation channels are not on it. The road is not on it. The space is blank.*

*I sent a letter to the provincial governor. I wrote that the Survey Office was destroying villages. I do not think he will read it.*

*They have me in a room here. The walls are white and empty. They remind me of the map. The blank space where V-4 used to be.*

*I have the stone. They let me keep it. They didn't think it was important.*

*I can still feel Haret's hand on mine. I can still smell the bread she made. I can still hear her laugh when I tried to explain what a map was.*

*But last night, I tried to remember the road to V-4, and I couldn't. I could see it clearly on Monday. I could draw it from memory. But now—*

*Now there's just a blank space where the memory was.*

*I looked at the stone and I could see her face. But I couldn't remember her name.*

*I am writing this because she said words might be safe.*

*My name is Emilia Voss. I was a cartographer. I drew maps of places I did not understand.*

*There was a village called V-4. There was a woman there who made bread. There were irrigation channels and a road and eighteen buildings and people who lived there and died there and were buried there.*

*There was a woman whose name I—*

*I have the stone. I can still see her face. I can still—*

*The stone is cold now.*

**TRANSCRIPT OF INTERVIEW**
**Subject:** Emilia Voss, patient #4471
**Interviewer:** Dr. Frederick Marsden, Alemere Sanatorium
**Dated:** 15 October 1923
**Subject's condition:** Agitated but coherent

MARSDEN: Miss Voss, can you tell me why you're here?

VOSS: I erased a village.

MARSDEN: You tore up a map.

VOSS: Yes. I tore it up because I was trying to stop the erasure. But it was already too late. The decision had been made. The map was just the formality.

MARSDEN: What village do you believe you erased?

VOSS: I didn't erase it. I drew it. I drew it carefully. I included it. But then my supervisor told me to remove it and I—

*[Long pause]*

MARSDEN: Take your time.

VOSS: There were eighteen buildings. There was a woman who made bread. She had a garden. She said the land remembers.

MARSDEN: And where is this village now?

VOSS: *[Subject becomes visibly distressed]* I don't know. I can't remember the road anymore. I can't remember where it was. I have this stone—

*[Subject produces a small carved stone from pocket]*

MARSDEN: May I see it?

VOSS: No. No, you can't draw it. You can't make a picture. The pictures are how it happens.

MARSDEN: I won't draw it. I just want to look.

VOSS: *[Hands over stone, watches intently]* The land remembers. Even when the picture forgets. The land remembers.

MARSDEN: It's a stone.

VOSS: *[Subject begins weeping]* It's all that's left. Can't you see? It's all that's left and soon I won't even remember why I'm holding it.

MARSDEN: Miss Voss, I want to help you. But I need you to understand that maps don't erase villages. Maps are just—

VOSS: *[Suddenly calm]* How many settlements were in the eastern province before your office drew it?

MARSDEN: I don't understand the question.

VOSS: You have a map of this region in the Atlas. Third Edition. 1915. How many settlements does it show?

MARSDEN: I don't have that information.

VOSS: Find it. Count them. Then go to the eastern province and count the villages that are actually there. And then ask yourself what happened to the ones on the map that you can't find on the ground.

*[Long silence]*

MARSDEN: I'll make a note of your concern.

VOSS: Don't write it down. Don't draw it. Just go and look. Just walk the land and count.

**LETTER**
**From:** Dr. Frederick Marsden, Alemere Sanatorium
**To:** Director Aldous Crane, Meridian Colonial Survey Office
**Dated:** 3 December 1923
**Re:** Patient #4471 (Voss, Emilia)

Director Crane,

I am writing regarding the condition of your former employee, Miss Emilia Voss.

Miss Voss has been in our care since September. Her condition has not improved. She remains convinced that her cartographic work resulted in the destruction of several villages, and she refuses to participate in any activity involving drawing or writing.

I am writing because, in the course of her treatment, Miss Voss made several requests that I verify her claims. Specifically, she asked me to compare the settlement counts on your survey maps against the actual terrain.

I have done so.

I have obtained copies of the Meridian Colonial Atlas, First through Third Editions (1863, 1889, 1915), and have compared them with your Fourth Edition, which includes Miss Voss's Lake Veld map.

Director Crane:

The First Edition shows forty-seven settlements in the eastern province.

The Second Edition shows thirty-one.

The Third Edition shows twenty-two.

The Fourth Edition shows fifteen.

I am not a cartographer. I am not a surveyor. I understand that boundaries shift, that villages are consolidated, that names change. I understand that the land is not the map.

But I sent a colleague to the eastern province last month. He counted fifteen settlements.

There should be more. There were more. The records show more. The first surveyors counted forty-seven villages. Now there are fifteen.

I am not suggesting what Miss Voss suggests. I am not a superstitious man. But I would very much like an explanation, and I cannot find one in your records.

I note that your office has recently begun surveys of the Northern Province. I note that the preliminary reports list sixty-three settlements.

Director Crane: how many will the next edition show?

Please advise.

Dr. Frederick Marsden

**MEMORANDUM**
**To:** All Meridian Colonial Survey Offices
**From:** Office of the Governor, Eastern Province
**Dated:** 10 June 1924
**Re:** Suspension of Survey Activities

By order of the Provincial Governor, all survey and cartographic activities in the Eastern and Northern Provinces are suspended pending review.

Survey teams are to return to Alemere immediately. All draft maps, field notes, and related materials are to be secured in the Survey Office archives.

No maps are to be printed, distributed, or altered.

This order is effective immediately and will remain in force until further notice.

Governor T. Craven
