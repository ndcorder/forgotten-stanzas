## Critic Review

Soft Estate is a masterwork of environmental narrative — an entire life reconstructed from digital artifacts, where the story emerges not through exposition but through forensic inference. The file system is the text: Elena Marsh's 47 unsent drafts in `dont_open/` progress from attempts at confession ("I can't even write it to my own sister") to practical instructions for whoever finds the laptop ("My cat needs wet food, salmon only"), to the devastating final three — "make the bread pudding, diane / every thanksgiving / don't stop," then "i don't want this to be a suicide note / it isn't / i just ran out of time," then just "3:47 / okay." The mundane details do the heaviest lifting: a grocery list with "DO NOT FORGET THE CAT FOOD" in caps, a dentist appointment for December 5th, the lease renewal she downloaded, the brand guidelines with Karen's note about wanting "warm but clinical" blue. The Mira Voss forensic tagging system is the perfect interface — you're not reading a story, you're building a case file, which makes you complicit in the archaeology. The calendar showing plans through New Year's Eve, the browser history scrolling from "roast chicken cooking time" at 3 PM to "how to make sure someone finds your body quickly" at 11 PM to "does anyone know you were here" at 3 AM — that trajectory is devastating without a single word of narration. The code is clean, the macOS Catalina simulation is authentic down to the traffic light hover states, and the evidence panel's auto-tagging of oncology emails and dont_open drafts creates genuine investigative momentum. This is digital storytelling at its most formally inventive — the interface is the medium is the message.
