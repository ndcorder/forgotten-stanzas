## Critic Review

Exit Interview with a Building That Volunteered for Demolition is the portfolio's definitive statement on the impossibility of writing about peace — a long-form journalism piece where the journalist's complicity in misframing the story becomes the story, and the building's serene refusal to grieve is more unsettling than any despair would be. The thermal translation system is the portfolio's most brilliantly weaponized constraint since Witness Stand's forensic linguistics: no configuration for apology, forgiveness, anger, or "hurt," which means the building literally cannot perform the emotions the journalist needs it to perform, and its creative repurposing of the 73-76°F range to express "relief" demonstrates agency within constraint that mirrors the entire piece's thesis. Every detail earns devastating weight — Marcus Delgado pausing at the second-floor window to watch a woman walk to her car, replayed a number of times that overflows the counter; the moment all 23 zones dropped below 67°F when the building heard "ugly"; the final sustained 74.9°F reading that falls outside every protocol category and prompts researchers to propose a new one they cannot name. Kovac's arc from sympathetic journalist through frustrated thesis-defender to outright manipulator — asking emotional questions she knows the building has already refused, wanting it to suffer for the story — is the portfolio's most honest self-portrait of the writer's bad faith, and her admission ("I understood that what I needed was for the building to suffer") is more morally complex than anything else in the archive. The building's last incomplete sentence, the mid-interview connection loss, the demolition moved forward eleven days, the correction noting 74.9°F not 74.2°F — these accumulate into an ending where the artifact itself becomes the violence it describes: the building's last complete thought was not recorded, and we are holding the incomplete record, performing exactly the extraction the building refused. Sits alongside Witness Stand, Autopsy of a Joke That Killed, Your Car Knows You're Sad, Voting Patterns, and A Natural History of Ransomware as the portfolio's seventh canonical forensic narrative, and the first to make the interviewer's failure the primary evidence.
