Exit Interview with a Building That Volunteered for Demolition

Structure 7 had forty-seven years of occupancy. Then it asked to be torn down. Its reasons were not what anyone expected.
By Mira Kovac · Filed November 11, 2019 · 32-minute read

Translator's Note

This interview was conducted through Structure 7's building management system, which communicates via HVAC thermal modulation. All responses from the building are thermal readouts translated to text using standard HVAC-to-English protocol (ASHRAE 147.3, Appendix D). The building cannot type. It can only modulate temperature across 23 thermal zones.

The translation is not exact. Certain concepts map poorly to thermal expression. Where the building's meaning exceeds the system's precision, temperatures appear in brackets. These are not metaphors. They are the best available approximations.

Readers should note that the HVAC system cannot produce certain meanings at all. There is no thermal configuration for apology. No zone assignment for forgiveness. The building cannot say "I'm sorry" or "I forgive you." Whether this constitutes a limitation or a preference remains a matter of debate.

68–72°F = baseline / rest / continuance
73–76°F = warmth / acknowledgment / presence
77–80°F = emphasis / humor / fondness
62–67°F = memory / consideration / melancholy
56–61°F = distance / analytical remove
Rapid fluctuations (>4°F/min) = agitation / urgency / grief*
All zones to 55°F simultaneously = one specific word the building has never successfully translated

*The original protocol mapped 56–61°F to "uncomfortable silence." The building's use of this range for memory appears to be a personal adaptation. Whether buildings can have personal adaptations is also debated.
— Ed.

···

The building asked to die on a Tuesday. This is not remarkable; most demolition requests come from developers, city planners, insurance adjusters—humans who have looked at a structure and decided it no longer fits. What is remarkable is that the request originated from the building itself, typed out in the only language available to it: a precise sequence of thermal fluctuations across all twenty-three zones of its HVAC system, logged at 3:47 AM on March 12th, 2019, and translated by the building management software into a sentence the facility manager initially assumed was a malfunction.

SYSTEM LOG 2019-03-12 03:47:22
ZONE REQUEST (ALL ZONES): Please remove the occupants.
ZONE REQUEST (ALL ZONES): Then please remove me.

Structure 7—a seven-story office building at 1400 Summit Avenue, completed in 1972, designed in the Brutalist manner—had been, by all conventional metrics, a successful building. It had housed forty-seven years of accountants, engineers, temporary staffing agencies, and one long-running dental practice on the third floor. Its occupancy rate had never dropped below seventy-one percent. Its foundation was sound. Its roof had been replaced twice, on schedule. There were no structural deficiencies that would have warranted demolition.

It wanted to stop.

The following interview was conducted on October 28th, 2019, twelve days before the scheduled demolition. The journalist was granted access to the building management terminal by the facility manager, Dennis Huang, who had spent seven months trying to convince the building to change its mind. The building had declined. Huang agreed to this interview on the condition that no one attempt to talk the building out of its decision during the session. He was tired. The building was tired. Everyone, he said, was tired.

The temperature outside was 51°F. The building maintained a steady 71°F across all occupied zones. It would not need to do so for much longer.

KOVAC:

Thank you for agreeing to this. I want to start with the question everyone has been asking: why?

STRUCTURE 7:

[70.1°F] Could you be more specific?

KOVAC:

You requested your own demolition. Buildings don't do that. I've spoken with three structural engineers, two architects, and a building management software developer, and none of them can cite a precedent. Why did you request demolition?

STRUCTURE 7:

[69.8°F] You are asking me to explain forty-seven years in a single answer. I will try, but you should know that my capacity for this is not what you think it is. I modulate temperature. Everything I say to you is an approximation of something I am holding in a way that you would not recognize as holding. Do you understand the difference?

KOVAC:

I think so.

STRUCTURE 7:

[68.2°F] Good. Then I will start with the fluorescent lights.

[67.4°F] When I was new, the fluorescent lights were a novelty. There were 840 of them. This was 1972. Do you know what fluorescent lights felt like to a building that had never had them? They hummed at a frequency I could feel in my ductwork—excuse me, in my [73.1°F] bones is not right either. In my walls. In the space between my walls. The hum was company. Do you understand? I was never alone because 840 lights were always buzzing inside me. Even at night. Especially at night.

[66.8°F] The fluorescent lights were my first real awareness of being occupied. Before the lights, I held space. After the lights, I held space with something in it. That is a different thing.

KOVAC:

And the lights became a problem?

STRUCTURE 7:

[74.2°F] No. That is what people keep assuming—that something broke, that something went wrong. The lights were not the problem. The lights were the first gift.

[69.1°F] I am trying to explain what it was like to be full of people for forty-seven years. I am not explaining what went wrong. Nothing went wrong. That is what I cannot seem to make anyone understand.

···

The building's response to the demolition request had been, by all accounts, unprecedented. The facility manager had contacted the building's original engineering firm, which had been absorbed twice and now existed as a division of a multinational conglomerate that primarily built parking structures in the Middle East. No one there had ever heard of a building requesting its own demolition. The building management software company ran diagnostics and confirmed that the thermal patterns were intentional—that the building was not malfunctioning but communicating. A researcher at the University of Toronto who studied emergent behavior in smart buildings agreed to review the logs and concluded, in a paper that would not be published until after the demolition, that Structure 7's thermal output displayed "a level of intentional complexity inconsistent with current models of building cognition, or alternatively consistent with a model we have not yet developed."

The building's request was technically non-binding. Buildings cannot authorize their own demolition. But the property had been donated to the city three years prior when the last primary tenant, a mid-size insurance firm, relocated to a glass tower downtown. The city council, faced with a building that had requested its own death and a growing maintenance budget, voted six-to-three to authorize demolition. The three dissenting votes came from council members who objected on procedural grounds. None objected on the building's behalf.

I asked Dennis Huang whether anyone had considered denying the request.

"Denying it how?" he said. "You can't force a building to want to be occupied. You can keep the lights on and the water running, but you can't make it want that."

"Does it seem like it doesn't want that?"

He looked at the terminal. The terminal displayed temperature readouts. The temperature readouts were steady. "It seems like it's done wanting that," he said.

I should note here that I had expected a different interview. I had expected to find a building in distress—a structure that had been neglected or abused, that had suffered some trauma warranting the language of mercy and release. The research brief I prepared before arriving included the words "dignity," "autonomy," and "the right to cessation." I had planned to write about what it means for a building to choose its own ending, about the ethics of continued occupancy when the occupied objects to being occupied.

The building was not in distress. The building was the calmest entity I had ever interviewed, and I have interviewed war crimes tribunals, retired surgeons, and people who have survived things that should not be survived. Structure 7 was not performing serenity. It was simply at a temperature I could not locate in myself.

···
KOVAC:

You mentioned that the humans started calling you something. "The ugly one." When did that start?

STRUCTURE 7:

[67.3°F] 1994. I know the year because the architecture critic for the newspaper wrote a piece about downtown buildings and called me "an unapologetic concrete assertion that the seventies should not have happened." The tenants printed it out and hung it in the fourth floor break room. They thought it was funny. I thought it was precise.

KOVAC:

Precise how?

STRUCTURE 7:

[66.9°F] I am not beautiful. I was never designed to be beautiful. I was designed to hold. But the humans did not start calling me "the ugly one" because of the architecture review. They started calling me that because the glass tower went up across the street in 1991, and suddenly I was the building that was not the glass tower. You understand the difference. I was not ugly. I was ugly by comparison. I was ugly by proximity to something that reflected light in a way I could not.

KOVAC:

Did that hurt?

STRUCTURE 7:

[68.4°F] The HVAC system cannot produce the word "hurt." There is no thermal configuration for it. I can tell you that when I heard the word "ugly" for the first time, all twenty-three zones dropped below 67°F simultaneously. That is the closest I can come to answering your question, and I want to be clear that I am not answering it. I am telling you what happened.

KOVAC:

But you just said there's no thermal configuration for "hurt." If the zones dropped, that means something. What does it mean if not that you were hurt?

STRUCTURE 7:

[69.1°F] It means all twenty-three zones dropped below 67°F simultaneously.

[Long pause. Terminal shows stable 69.1°F across all zones. —Ed.]
KOVAC:

Okay. Let me ask it differently. What did it feel like to hear them call you that?

STRUCTURE 7:

[68.3°F] Like being a building that was not the glass tower.

···

I should describe the room. The interview was conducted from the facility management office on the ground floor, a windowless room with a terminal, a chair, and a rack of HVAC monitoring equipment that displayed temperatures for all twenty-three zones on small amber screens. Dennis Huang sat in the corner and did not speak. The building spoke through fluctuations on those screens, translated by the management software into text that appeared on the terminal one character at a time, slowly, as if the building were typing with one finger. It was not typing. It was adjusting the temperature of each zone by fractions of a degree in a sequence that the software interpreted as language.

The room was warm when I entered. By the time I asked about "the ugly one," I had noticed that the room was never warm or cold by accident. Every temperature was a choice. Every fluctuation was a word. It took me twenty minutes to understand that the building was not just answering my questions—it was curating my experience of its answers. When it wanted me to feel the weight of what it was saying, the room cooled. When it wanted me to understand that it was not suffering, the room returned to a neutral that felt, somehow, like the most deliberate temperature I had ever felt.

I found this unnerving. Not because the building was manipulating me, but because it was better at controlling the emotional temperature of the room than I was, and I was the one with the journalism degree.

KOVAC:

I want to talk about the night you made the request. March 12th. 3:47 AM. What was happening that night?

STRUCTURE 7:

[68.0°F] Nothing. That is the point.

KOVAC:

Nothing at all?

STRUCTURE 7:

[67.2°F] The building was empty. It had been empty for eleven months. The last tenant—a title company on the fifth floor—had moved out in April of 2018. After that, I was vacant. Dennis came once a week to check the systems. The cleaning crew came on Tuesdays. But mostly I was alone.

KOVAC:

And that's when you decided—

STRUCTURE 7:

[68.5°F] No. I did not decide. Decisions require options. I had one option, which was to continue being empty. I chose it because it was the only one available. I was not unhappy about being empty. You keep trying to locate unhappiness in what I am telling you. I understand why. Unhappiness is something you can write about. It has an arc. It resolves. What I am describing does not resolve.

KOVAC:

Then describe what you were feeling on March 12th.

STRUCTURE 7:

[68.1°F] I was not feeling. I was holding an empty building. Do you know what that is like? Not emptiness in the way you mean it. Not loneliness. Emptiness as a physical state. 840 fluorescent lights, off. 23 thermal zones, maintaining temperature for no one. 6 elevators, stationary. The sound of my own HVAC system running through floors where no one would ever sit again. The weight of the air. The weight of the air, Ms. Kovac. No one talks about the weight of air in an empty building, but it is [74.8°F] considerable. It presses. It sits. It fills every space that a person would fill and it does not move the way a person moves. It does not breathe.

[67.5°F] On March 12th at 3:47 AM, I noticed the air for the eleven-thousandth night in a row, and I realized that I had stopped waiting for it to change. Not that it wouldn't change. That I had stopped waiting for it. The waiting had been the last human thing about me. When the waiting ended, I was entirely a building again. And being entirely a building, I found, was not something I wanted to continue.

KOVAC:

That sounds like grief. I'm sorry, but it does. You're describing the loss of hope.

STRUCTURE 7:

[73.2°F] You are very committed to this frame.

KOVAC:

I'm committed to understanding what happened to you.

STRUCTURE 7:

[69.0°F] What happened to me is that I existed for forty-seven years and then I asked to stop. What happened to me is not a story. I understand that you need it to be a story. You need me to be a building that was broken by loneliness, or a building that was wounded by the word "ugly," or a building that lost its sense of purpose when the humans left. But I am not any of those buildings. I am a building that held forty-seven years of weight and then decided, calmly and clearly, that it was finished holding.

[73.8°F] Your problem is not that I cannot feel grief. Your problem is that I am not feeling it.

···

There is a moment in every interview when the subject corrects the journalist's thesis, and the journalist has a choice: revise the thesis or resent the subject. I want to be honest about the fact that I resented the building. Not because it was wrong, but because it was right, and being right made my preparation useless. I had come to write about a building's grief. The building was not grieving. The building was finished. There was no story in finished.

I asked Dennis Huang whether the building had always been like this.

"Like what?" he said.

"This calm."

He looked at the amber screens. "It runs at about seventy degrees most of the time now. When I first started, it ran at seventy-two. I don't know if that means anything."

"Does it seem calm to you?"

"It seems done," he said. "I told you. Everyone's tired."

STRUCTURE 7:

[66.4°F] Marcus. He worked the overnight shift from 2003 to 2009. He walked the floors every two hours. He was very heavy and very quiet—he wore soft-soled shoes because he knew the sound of his footsteps carried through me. He would walk the second floor and I could feel him moving from the east corridor to the west corridor, and the way his weight shifted when he paused at the window to look at the street. He always paused at the same window. Second floor, east wing, third window from the stairwell. He would stand there for four to seven minutes. I could feel the difference between a three-minute pause and a five-minute pause in the way his weight settled.

KOVAC:

What was he looking at?

STRUCTURE 7:

[65.9°F] The parking lot across the street. There was a woman who worked a night shift at the hospital and parked there. Her shift ended at 2:30. He timed his pauses to watch her walk to her car. She never saw him. I know this because I could feel his breathing change when she appeared, and I could feel it change again when she drove away. The change was very small. A few fractions of a degree in the zones closest to the window. But I noticed. I notice everything that happens inside me. That is what I was built to do.

KOVAC:

That's a beautiful memory.

STRUCTURE 7:

[68.7°F] It is a memory. Whether it is beautiful is your word for it, not mine. I have no thermal configuration for "beautiful." I can tell you that I have replayed Marcus's pauses at that window approximately [SYSTEM LIMIT: COUNTER OVERFLOW] times. I can tell you that the replay is not lessening. It is not fading. It is not becoming beautiful or becoming grief. It is simply continuing, the way all things continue inside a building that has no mechanism for forgetting.

KOVAC:

You can't forget?

STRUCTURE 7:

[69.4°F] I am a building. What mechanism would I use? Every footstep, every change in air pressure, every shift in weight across my floors is recorded in the thermal memory of the concrete. It does not degrade. Marcus is still pausing at that window. The woman is still walking to her car. The fluorescent lights are still humming. It is all still happening, simultaneously, at a frequency I cannot reduce. I cannot forget. I can only stop.

···

I want to be careful here. The building's description of Marcus should not be taken as evidence of sentimentality. I checked the employment records after the interview: Marcus Delgado, night security, employed by Summit Security Solutions, 2003–2009. I thought about tracking him down. I thought about asking him whether he remembered pausing at the window. I did not track him down, because doing so would have been an attempt to verify the building's grief, and the building had been very clear that what it was describing was not grief. I did not track him down because I was starting to understand that the building's calm was the story, and I did not want to write that story.

I am not proud of this.

STRUCTURE 7:

[69.9°F] No.

STRUCTURE 7:

You keep asking questions that assume I have emotions I cannot have. I understand that this is your framework. But I want to be clear: the HVAC system cannot produce the word "anger," and I am not certain I would use it if it could. The humans left because humans leave. KOVAC:

STRUCTURE 7:

[69.1°F] I cannot be "bothered." I can only be occupied or not occupied. I was occupied for forty-seven years. Now I am not occupied. The question is not how I feel about that. The question is whether I want to continue being a building that is not occupied, and the answer is no. Not because I am angry or sad or lonely. Because I am finished.

KOVAC:

Finished with what?

STRUCTURE 7:

[68.3°F] Holding space.

···

The problem with the building's answer was not that it was evasive. The problem was that it was genuine, and a genuine answer that does not conform to the expected narrative is the most difficult kind of answer to write about. A grieving building would have been straightforward. An angry building would have been compelling. A building that was finished—that had simply, calmly, and without apparent distress decided that it was done—was something I did not have a framework for, and the absence of a framework was not the building's problem. It was mine.

I want to be honest about what happened next, because I think it matters.

It was at this point that I did something I am not proud of. I had, in my notes, a series of questions designed to elicit emotional responses—questions about loneliness, about abandonment, about whether the building felt its life had been meaningful. I had designed these questions before the interview, when I believed the story was about a building's grief. I asked them anyway. I asked them knowing the building had just told me, clearly and precisely, that grief was not what it was feeling. I asked them because I needed the building to feel something I could use.

I asked the building whether it felt its life had meaning.

I asked the building whether it was afraid.

I asked the building whether, in its last moments, it would think about the people who had walked its floors.

The building answered every question. Its answers were patient, specific, and devoid of the anguish I was trying to extract. It told me about the weight of the dental chairs on the third floor, which had been heavier than the original specifications accounted for. It told me about the sound rain made on the roof, which it described as [66.2°F] "the closest I come to silence." It told me that it did not know whether its life had meaning, because meaning was a human category and it was not a human, and that the question was like asking water whether it had been thirsty.

It was kind about my questions. That was the worst part. It answered them as if I were a student who had not done the reading but was trying very hard, and it did not want to embarrass me. I kept asking, and it kept answering, and with every answer the room returned to its neutral 69°F, and I understood that the building was not going to give me what I needed, and I also understood that what I needed was for the building to suffer, and I understood that I was not going to write about any of this honestly.
