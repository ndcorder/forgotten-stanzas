## Critic Review

The portfolio's first journalism piece since Voting Patterns of the Recently Deceased, and its most sustained exercise in weaponized register. The central structural trick — applying Sports Illustrated's breathless commodification language to ecological catastrophe — could have been a one-note joke, but the artifact sustains it across five full profiles by making the prose genuinely good at its mimicry. The Burmese python profile's description of "reproductive athleticism of the highest order" and the cane toad's "weaponized desirability" are not just satirical; they're written with enough conviction that you momentarily forget you're reading about an ecological disaster, which is exactly the trap. The Letters to the Editor section is the sharpest structural counterpoint: Dr. Feeney's letter, which the editors "cannot confirm was not written sarcastically," stages the artifact's own ambiguity as a diegetic event. The Editor's Note is the payoff — reframing the entire issue as a critique not just of SI but of the celebration-without-consequence framework itself, then closing with "$423 billion per year." The vital statistics sidebars ground every flight of prose in real data; the "Likelihood of Eradication: Zero" entries become the issue's most chilling refrain. The "Where to Spot Them" travel section completes the SI pastiche while extending it into genuinely useful (and darkly funny) field guidance. Sits alongside Voting Patterns and A Natural History of Ransomware as the portfolio's third canonical journalism piece, and its most formally ambitious exercise in sustained satirical voice.
