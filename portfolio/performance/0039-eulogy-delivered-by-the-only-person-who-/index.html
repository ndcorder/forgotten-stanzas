Performance Score
Single Performer · Empty Chapel

Eulogy Delivered by the Only Person Who Showed Up

For Maren Vogt, 1959–2024

Duration: approximately 18 minutes, if the heating holds.
Setting: a municipal chapel. Six rows of eight seats. Seats unoccupied.
Speaker: Gerald Hines, 74. Retired. Twenty-seven years at the central post office, nine years at Briers Books on Kenmare Street.

Production Notes

Space: The chapel should feel too large for its contents. Whatever the actual venue, the audience must sense the emptiness around the performer. If the performance space is small, use lighting to suggest walls that are further away than they are. The audience should feel the dimensions of a room built for a hundred that holds one.
Sound: The heating system is the second performer. It should be live — recorded is acceptable only if the venue has no comparable system. The audience must hear a real mechanical cycle: a furnace or boiler kicking on, running for ninety seconds, going quiet. This rhythm is the only thing in the room that doesn't care about the eulogy. The speaker does not acknowledge it.
Lighting: Overhead fluorescence only — the kind of institutional strip lighting that hums at a frequency just below conscious notice. The performer should not be spotlit. He should be lit the way a room like this lights a person: evenly, without sentiment, the way it would light a committee meeting. The audience is in the same light.
The Performer: should not be an actor in the usual sense. A person with a public speaking voice but not a performance voice. A retired teacher, perhaps, or someone accustomed to reading aloud to children. The audience must believe he would be delivering this speech even if they were not there. That he is not performing. That he showed up because someone had to.
Lights: House to half
The house lights dim to half. The heating system is already running. After twelve seconds it shuts off. The silence that follows is the first silence of the performance and should be allowed to become uncomfortable — five seconds, six, seven — before the speaker enters from the side door.

Gerald enters carrying a single folded sheet of paper. He has not rehearsed. He walks to the lectern, adjusts the microphone downward — it is set for someone taller — and unfolds the paper. He does not look at the empty seats. He looks at the paper.

Thank you for coming.

Beat

That's what you say, isn't it. At these.

My name is Gerald. Gerald Hines. I knew Maren from the bookshop. I worked Saturdays at Briers Books on Kenmare Street for the last nine years. Maren came in on Tuesdays. She was a Tuesday customer.

Always bought a newspaper from whatever country she was reading about that week. Sometimes she'd buy the Irish Times, but that was just — I think she just liked the Irish Times.

Beat

She read newspapers from places most people couldn't find on a map. She'd ask if we could order them. Briers — it's a small shop. We couldn't. So she'd bring them in herself, in her bag, and she'd sit in the corner with a coffee and read the whole thing front to back. Sometimes she'd read things aloud. Little things. A paragraph. A sentence.

Beat

She corrected my pronunciation of Sarajevo once. I'd been saying it wrong. She didn't make a thing of it. She just said it the right way and moved on. Sa-ra-JE-vo. I say it right now. Every time.

The heating system begins its cycle. The speaker pauses until the initial kick of the furnace subsides.

She had this — I don't know what you'd call it. A focus. When she was reading, she was somewhere else. Not in the bookshop. You could see it. Her eyes moving across the page and whatever she was reading, it was real to her. More real than the room. More real than me standing behind the counter. Which was fine. I mean — that's just how she was.

Beat

I don't actually know what she did. For a living, I mean. I know it was something abroad. She traveled a lot. I could tell by the newspapers. She'd come in after being gone for weeks and you could see it on her. Not — I don't mean in a bad way. She just looked like she'd been somewhere real.

He folds the paper in half, then unfolds it again. He has not reached the bottom.

She had a particular look when she came in after a trip. Like she was carrying something that weighed a certain amount and she was used to the weight but it was still there. You know? Heavy in the arms but you don't put it down because you've been holding it so long you've forgotten it's heavy. It just is.

Sound: Heating cycles off
Three seconds

She never talked about what she did over there. Not really. We talked about books. The weather. Whether the buses were running. Small things. I liked that about her. She was easy to talk to. She listened. Most people don't listen, but she did.

Long beat

I said I don't know what she did. I should say — I didn't know until this week. When the funeral home called. They had me listed as a contact. I don't know why. Maybe I'm the only one she listed. I can't — anyway. They told me she was a correspondent. For the news. A war correspondent, they said.

He glances at the empty rows again. Briefly.

Which — I mean, that explains a lot. The newspapers. The trips. The way she'd sometimes look at the bookshop door when it opened, like she was expecting it to be someone, or like she was glad it wasn't. The way she flinched at loud sounds but only sometimes. Only certain kinds of loud.

Beat

She came back from places and didn't talk about what she saw there. Which is how you know it was bad.

Beat

She made other people's lives visible. That's what it means, I think. That's what the obituary said. She went places and she wrote about what happened there so the rest of us could see it. Could know. And then she came to my bookshop on Tuesdays and bought a newspaper and sat in the corner and was quiet. And that was the same person.

Same person.

Five seconds
The heating system runs. Gerald stands at the lectern. He looks at his paper but does not speak. The furnace noise fills the chapel.

Two seconds

There was one Tuesday — it was raining. Pouring. And she came in completely soaked. Hair, jacket, everything. And she sat down in the corner with her newspaper and I brought her a towel from the back. I said you'll catch your death. That's what you say. I said you'll catch your death. And she looked at me — and she laughed. She really laughed. She said Gerald, not likely. She said she'd been in worse.

Beat

And I said, worse than this rain? And she just — she smiled. She said, yes. Much worse than this rain.

His voice catches. He pauses. He presses the paper flat against the lectern with both hands.

I think about that a lot. That I said that to her. You'll catch your death. To someone who'd been — to someone who'd seen — and she just laughed. She was kind about it.

Long beat

The bookshop is still there. Kenmare Street. I'm not there anymore — I retired from the shop two years ago. My knees. But I go by sometimes. Stand outside. Look in the window.

Beat

Tuesday last week I went by. She wasn't there. Obviously. But I stood there for a while anyway. Like maybe she would be.

The heating system begins its cycle.

I don't know why I'm the only one here. I've been thinking about it all week. Why me. Why not — there must have been other people. Colleagues. People she worked with. People she met in all those places. People whose — whose deaths she made visible. You'd think someone would come. You'd think the chapel would be full.

Beat

But here we are.

He stops. The furnace noise fills the room. For the first time, Gerald becomes aware of the chapel's acoustic. He speaks the next line and hears his voice come back to him from the walls.

It has a nice echo, this place.

Beat

You can hear it on the consonants.

He listens to the room.

That's something.

Three seconds
Sound: Heating cycles off
The silence that follows is the longest silence in the performance. Eight seconds. The heating system has stopped. Nothing in the room is making sound. Gerald stands at the lectern. He looks at his paper one last time, then folds it.

I brought the wrong newspaper. I wanted to bring one of hers. One of the foreign ones. But I didn't know which — I didn't know which country. I didn't know which one to bring. So I brought the Irish Times. Because I remembered she liked it. Just because she liked it.

Beat

I left it in the back. On the table. If anyone wants it.

Long beat

Rest in peace, Maren.

He nods once. He does not say amen. He did not know if she would have wanted him to.

Gerald picks up his paper. He walks to the side door. He does not look at the empty rows on his way out. The door closes behind him.

A note on the echo: The speaker's observation that the chapel has a nice echo is not a theatrical beat. It is a man alone in a room, speaking aloud, noticing that the room is listening. He notes it because it is true and because noting true things was what Maren did, and he learned that from her without knowing he was learning it. He does not comment on it again. He continues, because he is speaking to her, and she is no longer in the room, and the echo is not her, and he knows this. The echo is the echo. It is enough that the walls hold the shape of what he said for a moment longer than he said it. He does not need the walls to be her. He needs to say the thing. He has said it. The sound will dissipate. That is what sound does. That is what the living do: we speak into rooms that will not remember us, and we do it anyway, because someone should.
A note on the empty seats: Under no circumstances should this score be performed with an audience seated in the chapel seats. The audience — the readers of this score — occupy the position of the empty room. They are the walls. They are the echo. They are the silence the speaker speaks into. They are not his audience. Maren is his audience. The readers of this score are witnesses to the fact that no one else came. That distinction matters. That is the difference between performance and testament.
