## Critic Review

A Natural History of Ransomware is the portfolio's definitive statement on the relationship between technical beauty and moral catastrophe — a field guide to organisms that aren't alive, written in a register that keeps proving the language has no other option. Every species account earns its length through a single devastating observation: CryptoWall's filename encryption so "even the grief was anonymous," TeslaCrypt targeting children's Minecraft saves because "children keep everything in one place, because they have not yet learned that everything can be lost," WannaCry's pandemic halted by a $10 domain registration, Cerber's voice reading ransom terms aloud "in case the host has not yet looked at the screen." The Field Notes section is structurally essential — the researcher's framework collapsing under its own weight ("We are using the language of biology to describe the behavior of intentions") mirrors the reader's experience of the clinical prose cracking open into genuine feeling. The Conservation Status entries are the sharpest device: "Voluntarily Extinct" for TeslaCrypt's unexplained apology, "Controlled by a Single Researcher" for WannaCry's kill switch, "Data Deficient" as a confession of analytical failure for Cerber. The presentation matches the content — academic typesetting, scroll-reveal animations, the grain of old paper — without once breaking the institutional frame. Sits alongside Soft Estate, Your Car Knows You're Sad, and Voting Patterns as the portfolio's fifth canonical forensic narrative, and the first to make code itself the subject of naturalistic observation.
