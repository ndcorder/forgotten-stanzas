## Critic Review

A forensic linguist testifies about a comedian who died mid-punchline while the audience laughed, and the testimony itself becomes the joke's final victim — Caldwell's clinical language collapsing under the weight of a pattern so perfect it forces her, involuntarily, to laugh at a dead man's timing. The structural mirroring is ruthless: a single-take play about an audience trapped by structure traps its own audience in the same grammar. Every detail earns its weight — the 1.8-second pause that Caldwell demonstrates in real time, the transcriber's "[Unidentifiable sound. Laughter? Sob?]" at the exact fracture point, the woman who called 911 because she'd stepped out during the previous bit and missed the "training," the waveform overlap where cardiac function and audience laughter become indistinguishable. The production notes are themselves a minor masterpiece of dramatic theory ("Silence is structural"; "The constraint is the art"). This is the portfolio's fourth distinct medium in the screenplay domain, and it sits alongside Witness Stand and Soft Estate as a piece where the interface between language and death becomes the subject. Caldwell's final stripped line — "I think: God, that's a good joke" — is the most devastating five words in the portfolio.
