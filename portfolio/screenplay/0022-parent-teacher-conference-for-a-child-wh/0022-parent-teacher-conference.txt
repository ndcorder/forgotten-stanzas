PARENT-TEACHER CONFERENCE

Written for the screen


INT. ELEMENTARY SCHOOL - CLASSROOM 2B - NIGHT

A second-grade classroom after hours. Construction paper cutouts hang from the ceiling. A poster on the wall reads "OUR CLASSROOM COMMUNITY" with construction-paper faces glued around it. Twenty-three faces. Uneven scissors work.

Desks arranged in clusters of four. On each desk: a name written in Sharpie on masking tape. Some peeling.

The teacher's desk sits near the window, covered in neat stacks of folders, a mug that says "WORLD'S OKAYEST TEACHER," a tape dispenser, a jar of sharpened pencils.

ELAINE PEARSON (40s, cardigan, the specific exhaustion of someone who has been smiling since 7 AM) sits behind the desk. She checks her watch. She consults a printed schedule, crosses off a name.

The door opens.

CAROL ANOAKI (40s, leather jacket over a blouse, hair gathered up in a clip that's losing the fight) enters. She carries a manila folder, a Barnes & Noble bag, and the particular tension of someone who rehearsed what she was going to say.

                                    ELAINE
                      Mrs. Anoaki, hi. Please, sit down.

                                    CAROL
                            (sitting, adjusting the chair)
              Thank you. I'm sorry, I know you have a lot of
              parents tonight.

                                    ELAINE
                      Oh, it's fine. I'm just glad you could make
                      it. I know work makes it—

                                    CAROL
                      It does. It really does. But I wanted to
                      come. I've been wanting to talk to you.

Elaine opens a file folder. Inside: a printed class list, clipped together. She pages to the middle, finds what she's looking for, and clicks her pen.

                                    ELAINE
                      So. Emily. How are things at home? She's
              such a bright girl.

                                    CAROL
                            (relieved)
              That's—that's actually exactly what I wanted to
              talk about. She's been so... I don't know. Different
              lately. Quieter.

                                    ELAINE
                      Hmm. I haven't noticed her being quieter,
              exactly. She's always been a little—

                                    CAROL
                      Reserved?

                                    ELAINE
                      I was going to say "watchful." Like she's
              paying attention to things other kids don't.

Carol nods. She opens her manila folder.

                                    CAROL
              That's exactly it. That's the word. "Watchful."
              She's been coming home and telling me things.

Elaine waits.

                                    CAROL (CONT'D)
              Like, last month, she told me she'd figured out
              what her science fair project was going to be. She
              was so excited.

                                    ELAINE
                      Oh God, yes. The volcano. Yes. She was
              absolutely certain about the volcano. She told me
              she'd been thinking about it since—

                                    CAROL
                      Thanksgiving.

                                    ELAINE
                            (slight pause)
              Halloween.

                                    CAROL
                      She said she wanted to do something about
              birds, actually. Migration patterns.

Elaine consults her notes.

                                    ELAINE
                      I have here it was a volcano. She turned in
              her proposal form—"Volcanoes: Why They Explode."
              She was very specific about the title.

                                    CAROL
                            (checking her folder)
              I have the proposal form she brought home. She
              was so proud of it.

  Carol finds a sheet of paper, unfolds it, and slides it across
the desk.

                                    CAROL (CONT'D)
              See? Right there.

Elaine looks at the paper. She looks at her notes. She looks at
the paper again.

                                    ELAINE
                      Huh.

                                    CAROL
                      What?

                                    ELAINE
                      This is definitely about birds.

                                    CAROL
                      Yes. Migration patterns. She was obsessed
              with the geese on our block.

                                    ELAINE
                      She told me—"baking soda and vinegar."
              Those exact words. She was very specific about
              wanting to see something "actually explode."

                                    CAROL
                            (small laugh)
              That doesn't sound like—

                      (stops herself)

              Well. Children are different at school.

                                    ELAINE
                            (nodding)
              That's very true. She might have changed her
              mind. Second graders change their minds.

                                    CAROL
              Exactly.

A beat. Carol puts the proposal form back in her folder. Elaine
makes a note on her pad.

                                    ELAINE
                      Okay. Well, regardless. She did a great job
              on the project. She got an A minus.

                                    CAROL
                      She got an A.

                                    ELAINE
                      A minus. I have it right here.

Elaine pages through her folder, finds a grade sheet, turns it
so Carol can see it.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              See?

                                    CAROL
                      I'm looking at her report card, and—

Carol opens her folder and produces a folded document.

                                    CAROL (CONT'D)
              A.

                                    ELAINE
                            (blinking)
              That's not the report card I issued.

                                    CAROL
              This came in the mail. In the school envelope.

                                    ELAINE
                      Those aren't—I print those from the school
              system. They're generated by the—

                                    CAROL
              I'm looking at it right now. A.

Elaine leans back.

                                    ELAINE
                      Okay. Let's move on. There was an incident
              last month I wanted to discuss.

                                    CAROL
              The playground.

                                    ELAINE
                      You heard about it.

                                    CAROL
              She came home crying. She said a boy pushed her.

                                    ELAINE
                      That's what she told me too. Marcus Chen.
              He pushed her during recess.

                                    CAROL
              In the chest. She had a bruise on her arm.

                                    ELAINE
                      In the back. She fell forward onto the
              wood chips.

                                    CAROL
              She showed me the bruise. Right here.

Carol touches her own forearm.

                                    CAROL (CONT'D)
              She said he grabbed her by the collar. By the
              basketball court.

                                    ELAINE
                      It was by the slide. She was standing in
              line for the slide. He bumped her from behind.

                                    CAROL
              She doesn't go near the slide. She's afraid of
              heights.

                                    ELAINE
                      She went down it three times last Tuesday.

                                    CAROL
              She's never been on that slide. Not once. Not
              since kindergarten.

Elaine reaches for her phone, unlocks it, scrolls through photos.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              Here.

She turns the phone toward Carol. Carol looks at it.

                                    CAROL
              That's not her.

                                    ELAINE
                      That's Emily. Standing right by the slide.

                                    CAROL
              That is not my daughter.

                                    ELAINE
                      It's Emily Anoaki.

                                    CAROL
              That girl is blonde.

                                    ELAINE
                      Emily has—

                      (stops)

Elaine looks at the photo.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              Emily has light brown hair.

                                    CAROL
              My daughter has black hair. Like mine.

Elaine looks at Carol's hair.

                                    ELAINE
                      Maybe... maybe she's washed out in the light.
              The sun was—

                                    CAROL
              She's not washed out. She's blonde. That is a
              different child.

                                    ELAINE
                      She is wearing Emily's jacket. The purple
              jacket with the cat on the back.

                                    CAROL
              I bought that jacket.

                                    ELAINE
                      Emily wore it every day in October.

                                    CAROL
              My daughter wore it every day in October. I bought
              it for her. At Target. On Hallowe—

                      (stops herself)

A strange look crosses her face.

                                    ELAINE
                      What?

                                    CAROL
              Nothing.

                                    ELAINE
                      You stopped.

                                    CAROL
              It's nothing.

Elaine sets down her pen.

                                    ELAINE
                      Mrs. Anoaki, I'm going to ask you something
              and I need you to bear with me.

                                    CAROL
                      Okay.

                                    ELAINE
                      When is Emily's birthday?

                                    CAROL
              March fourth.

                                    ELAINE
                      March nineteenth.

                                    CAROL
              She's my daughter. I was there.

                                    ELAINE
                      I have her birth certificate on file.

                                    CAROL
              So do I.

A beat.

                                    ELAINE
                      I sent home cupcakes. For her birthday. In
              March. She specifically asked for lemon with
              strawberry frosting.

                                    CAROL
              She asked for chocolate. With blue candles.

                                    ELAINE
                      She told me she doesn't like chocolate. One
              candle. Pink.

                                    CAROL
              She loves chocolate. She had chocolate cupcakes
              at her party. March fourth. With six blue candles.

                                    ELAINE
                      March nineteenth. Lemon. One pink candle.

                      (stops)

                      Let me check something.

Elaine turns to her computer monitor. She clicks, types, clicks
again.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              I'm looking at my class roster.

                                    CAROL
                      And?

                                    ELAINE
                      Emily Anoaki is not on it.

                                    CAROL
              That's impossible.

                                    ELAINE
                      I have twenty-three students. I'm looking at
              their names right now.

She turns the monitor so Carol can see. Carol scans the list.

                                    CAROL
              She's not—

                      (leaning closer)

                      She's not there.

                                    ELAINE
                      I'm looking at her folder. Her grades, her
              attendance. I have notes. I have her handwriting.

                      (holds up a paper)

                      Here.

                                    CAROL
              That's not—

                      (looks closer)

                      That's not her handwriting.

                                    ELAINE
                      You just said—

                                    CAROL
              My daughter has very neat handwriting. She works
              hard on it. That is—

Carol takes the paper from Elaine's hand.

                                    CAROL (CONT'D)
              That handwriting is a mess. The letters are all
              different sizes. Look at the 'E's.

                                    ELAINE
                      Emily's 'E's.

                                    CAROL
              My daughter's 'E's are perfect. She practices them.
              She has a notebook full of—

Carol opens her manila folder. She takes out a sheet of homework.
She places it next to the paper Elaine was holding.

                                    CAROL (CONT'D)
              Look.

Two papers, side by side.

One is neat. Careful. Letters aligned and uniform. The margins
filled with tiny drawings—birds, mostly. Wings repeated in
miniature.

The other is a child's scrawl. Uneven, hurried. The margins
completely blank.

Elaine picks up both papers.

                                    ELAINE
                      This doesn't make sense.

                                    CAROL
              Which one of those looks like the same child to
              you?

Elaine says nothing.

                                    ELAINE
                      This is a homework assignment. Spelling.
              "Write each word three times."

                      (reading from the neat paper)

                      Emily Anoaki. October twelfth.

                      (reading from the other)

                      Emily Anoaki. October twelfth.

She puts both papers down.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              These are not the same child.

                                    CAROL
              No.

                                    ELAINE
                      One of them is in my class. The other is—

                                    CAROL
              The other is mine.

A long beat.

                                    ELAINE
                      But which one?

Carol looks at Elaine. Elaine looks at Carol.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
                      You're sure your daughter goes to this school.

                                    CAROL
              Of course I'm sure. I drop her off every morning.

                                    ELAINE
                      At this school. Riverside Elementary.

                                    CAROL
              Riverside Elementary.

                                    ELAINE
                      Room 2B.

                                    CAROL
              Room 2B. Your classroom. I drop her off at your
              classroom door every morning.

                                    ELAINE
                      And I'm telling you there is no Emily Anoaki
              in my class.

                                    CAROL
              Then who have you been teaching? Who takes tests?
              Who got an A minus on the science fair?

                                    ELAINE
                      A girl named Emily. Who looks—

                      (gesturing at the photo)

                      Like that. Light brown hair. Doesn't like
                      chocolate. Won't go near the slide.

                                    CAROL
              That's not my daughter.

                                    ELAINE
                      Then whose daughter is it?

Silence.

                                    CAROL
              That's a photograph. From your phone.

                                    ELAINE
                      Yes.

                                    CAROL
              A girl wearing my daughter's jacket. The jacket
              I bought her.

                                    ELAINE
                      At Target.

                                    CAROL
              On Halloween.

Carol stops. That look again—the one she tried to hide before.

                                    ELAINE
                      What about Halloween?

                                    CAROL
              She said something. That night. She was putting on
              her costume. She wanted to be a cat.

                                    ELAINE
                      She was a cat.

                                    CAROL
              She was a pirate.

                                    ELAINE
                      She told me she was going to be a cat. She
              was very excited about painting her own—

                                    CAROL
              She said something about the substitute librarian.

                                    ELAINE
                            (pause)
              What about her?

                                    CAROL
              She said Ms. Fletcher was going to fall. On the
              front steps. On Wednesday.

                                    ELAINE
                      What do you mean "going to"?

                                    CAROL
              I mean she said it was going to happen. Before it
              happened. She was putting on her costume, and she
              said, "Ms. Fletcher is going to fall on the steps
              on Wednesday and hurt her wrist."

                                      ELAINE
                      And she did?

                                    CAROL
              Wednesday afternoon. Right in front of me. We were
              leaving the library and Ms. Fletcher fell on the
              front steps. She hurt her wrist.

                                    ELAINE
                      That's—

                                    CAROL
              I know what it sounds like.

A beat.

                                    ELAINE
                      Emily told me something, too. Around the same
              time.

                                    CAROL
              What did she tell you?

                                    ELAINE
                      We were doing show-and-tell. She brought in a
              rock. She said it was from her grandfather's garden.

                                    CAROL
              Her grandfather doesn't have a garden. He lives in
              an apartment.

                                    ELAINE
                      She said—"Ms. Fletcher is going to fall on the
              steps."

Carol stares at her.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              Those words. She told me on Monday. She said it
              would happen on Wednesday.

                                    CAROL
              My Emily said the same thing. On Monday.

                                    ELAINE
                      Two different girls. Both predicted the same
              fall. The same day.

                                    CAROL
              Both named Emily Anoaki.

                                    ELAINE
                      Yes.

                                    CAROL
              Neither one had met Ms. Fletcher before. She just
              started that week.

                                    ELAINE
                      No. Neither one.

They sit with this.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
                      Hold on.

Elaine stands and goes to the wall. She looks at the poster:
"OUR CLASSROOM COMMUNITY." She counts the faces.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              Twenty-three.

She goes back to her desk, looks at her computer screen.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              I have twenty-three students on my roster.

She looks at the poster.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              Twenty-three.

She counts again.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              Twenty-three faces.

She picks up the schedule from her desk.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              Twenty-three conferences.

She sits back down.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              You're conference number twenty-four.

                                    CAROL
              What?

                                    ELAINE
                      My schedule has twenty-three names. You're not
              on it.

                                    CAROL
              I got an email. From the school. From the automated
              system. It said "your parent-teacher conference is
              tonight at 7:45."

                                    ELAINE
                      I didn't send that. I scheduled twenty-three.

                                    CAROL
              I'm here about a daughter who isn't on your roster.
              Wearing a jacket that girl in your photo had. Sitting
              in a classroom she apparently doesn't attend.

                                    ELAINE
                      And yet here you are.

                                    CAROL
                      And yet here I am.

A beat.

                                    CAROL (CONT'D)
              Elaine.

                                    ELAINE
                      Yes.

                                    CAROL
              Where is my daughter?

                                    ELAINE
                      I don't know.

                                    CAROL
              Where is the girl in that photograph?

                                    ELAINE
                      I don't know that either.

                                    CAROL
              Two girls. Same name. Same prediction. Same jacket.
              Neither one—

A KNOCK at the door.

Both women freeze.

                                    ELAINE
                      I have another conference.

                                    CAROL
              You said there are twenty-three.

                                    ELAINE
                      There are.

Another knock.

                                    ELAINE (CONT'D)
              I should—

Elaine stands but doesn't move toward the door. She looks at
Carol. Carol looks at the two homework assignments still on the
desk. The neat one with the birds in the margins. The messy one
with blank white space where the birds should be.

                                    CAROL
              What did your Emily say? What else did she say about
              Wednesday?

                                    ELAINE
                      What did yours say?

                                    CAROL
              She said—

Another knock. Closer.

                                    ELAINE
                      What did she say?

                                    CAROL
              She said—

The door opens.

                                    MR. DELACROIX (O.S.)
              Mrs. Pearson? I'm here for my conference.

Elaine looks at Carol. Carol looks at the two homework assignments.

Carol picks up her folder. She puts the neat homework inside.
She stands.

                                    CAROL
                            (quiet)
              We're not finished.

                                    ELAINE
                            (quiet)
              No.

                                    CAROL
              I'll call you.

                                    ELAINE
                      You don't have my number.

                                    CAROL
                            (looking at her)
              Yes I do.

Carol exits past MR. DELACROIX (50s, weary, holding a paper bag
from a bakery).

                                    MR. DELACROIX
              Sorry I'm late. Traffic was—

                      (notices Elaine's expression)

                      Everything okay?

Elaine stares at the door Carol left through. On her desk: the
remaining homework assignment. The messy one. The margins blank.
She picks it up.

                                    ELAINE
                      Have a seat, Mr. Delacroix.

She puts the homework in the folder and closes it.

On the folder: a label. "ANOAKI, EMILY."

She clicks her pen.

In the hallway, Carol walks toward the exit. She stops. She
opens her folder and looks at the homework inside.

The neat handwriting. The careful letters. The margins full of
tiny birds.

She has never seen her daughter draw birds.

She stands in the empty hallway, holding homework completed by
a child she does not know.

Inside the classroom, Elaine clicks her pen again. And again.
Staring at the closed folder.

Waiting for a conference with a parent who isn't coming.

                                    FADE OUT.
