9. The recorder stopped because it was designed to stop when sound dropped below a certain threshold for a certain duration. I used to think this was a flaw — that it cut off what I most needed to hear. I no longer think so. The machine was more honest than I was. It recognised silence and did not try to translate it. I have spent eleven years doing what the recorder refused to do: projecting Bulgarian, Quechua, whale sound, mathematics, gesture into gaps that may contain nothing at all. Perhaps the silences are not texts. Perhaps they are just silences — intervals during which two people who once loved each other sat in the same house and did not speak, and the not-speaking meant nothing more than the cessation of effort. I don't believe that. But I can't prove it's wrong. The recording is 23 minutes and 44 seconds long. The total duration of silence is 555 seconds: 9 minutes and 15 seconds. The rest is ambient sound. Neither of them spoke once. I keep listening anyway. I don't know why. I don't know what I'm listening for. My mother is seventy-one. My father is seventy-four. They still live in that house. They still eat dinner at that table. The recorder is in a drawer in my apartment in Columbus. I haven't pressed play in four months. I will press it again. I always do.

Translator's Note

The translations in this document are, by definition, speculative. There is no source text in Bulgarian, Quechua, American Sign Language, mathematics, or whale song. There is only a digital recording of a kitchen in April 2019, and two people who once spoke to each other and then, for reasons neither ever explained to me, stopped.

The recording was made on a Sony ICD-PX470, purchased with birthday money, positioned under the table on a dish towel to dampen vibration. The battery lasted 49 hours. I used 24 minutes.

I have not shared this recording with anyone. I am sharing it now, in this form, because I no longer know what else to do with it.

— Columbus, Ohio. January 2030.
